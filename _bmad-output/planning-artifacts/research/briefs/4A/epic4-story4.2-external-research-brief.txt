RESEARCH BRIEF: Epic 4, Story 4.2 — External Dashboard & Visualization Research
================================================================================
Project:    Forex Pipeline
Epic:       4 — Pipeline Dashboard & Visualization
Story:      4.2 — External research on dashboard frameworks for chart-led quant dashboards
Date:       2026-03-19
For:        External researchers

PURPOSE
-------
We are building a browser-based dashboard for a personal forex backtesting
pipeline. The dashboard is where the operator decides whether a strategy is
worth risking real money on — or whether to kill it. Every view must be
designed to surface INSIGHT: "what is this strategy actually doing, and
should I trust it?"

This brief defines what to research and what deliverables we expect back.

The priority is analytical depth. We are NOT building a live trading monitor.
All data is generated by batch backtests and loaded from files. The challenge
is presenting that data so the operator can spot problems, understand behavior,
and make confident go/no-go decisions in minutes, not hours.


WHAT THE DASHBOARD MUST SHOW
-----------------------------
These are the functional requirements:

  FR62  Equity curves for backtest results
  FR63  Trade distribution charts (over time, by session, by outcome)
  FR64  Trade logs with key metrics per trade
  FR65  Pipeline stage status (strategy progression through a validation gauntlet)
  FR66  Temporal split markers on charts (in-sample / out-of-sample / forward-test)
  FR67  Walk-forward window results (per-window metrics, pass/fail)
  FR68  Confidence score breakdown (RED/YELLOW/GREEN per candidate strategy)

Non-functional:
  NFR6  Dashboard pages must load within 3 seconds for standard views

Architecture constraint:
  Backend is Python (FastAPI serving REST endpoints). Frontend is a browser SPA.
  All data comes from Arrow IPC files and SQLite. No streaming, no live feeds.

Data scale:
  - Equity curves: 300K-400K M1 data points per year per pair
  - Trade logs: tens to low hundreds of trades per backtest run
  - Walk-forward: 5-20 windows per optimization run
  - Multiple strategies at different stages of validation, compared side by side


THE INSIGHT QUESTION
====================
The central question is NOT "which library renders fastest" — it's:

  "How do the best platforms help a trader UNDERSTAND their backtest results?"

Every research area below should be approached through this lens. We want to
know what visualizations, layouts, and interaction patterns lead to genuine
analytical insight — the kind that prevents you from deploying a strategy
that looked good on paper but fails in production.

Specific insight challenges we need solved:

  a) REGIME AWARENESS — Can you see at a glance whether a strategy works in
     all market conditions or only thrives in one regime and bleeds in others?
     (e.g., trending markets vs. ranging, high-vol vs. low-vol periods)

  b) SESSION BEHAVIOR — Forex sessions (Asian, London, NY overlap) have
     radically different characteristics. Can you see per-session P&L, spread
     impact, and trade quality broken down visually?

  c) TEMPORAL STABILITY — Does the strategy degrade over time? Is the second
     half of the backtest worse than the first? Walk-forward windows should
     make this obvious without needing to read tables of numbers.

  d) COST SENSITIVITY — How much of the edge is eaten by spread and slippage?
     Is the strategy profitable only because the cost model is generous?
     Can you visualize gross vs. net equity to see the cost drag?

  e) TRADE CLUSTERING — Are trades evenly distributed or do they cluster
     around specific times/events? Clustering often indicates overfitting
     to specific market conditions.

  f) DRAWDOWN ANATOMY — Not just "max drawdown was X%" but: how long did it
     last, how many trades occurred during it, was recovery gradual or sudden,
     and does the drawdown pattern suggest the strategy broke or just had
     a bad run?

  g) COMPARISON — When evaluating parameter variants or strategy versions
     side by side, what layout makes differences immediately obvious?


RESEARCH AREAS
==============

1. ESTABLISHED TRADING PLATFORMS — Insight Study
-------------------------------------------------
Study how these platforms present backtest results. Focus on what actually
helps a trader make decisions, not just what looks pretty.

Platforms to study:

  a) MetaTrader 5 (MT5)
     - Strategy Tester results tab (equity curve, balance line, deposit load)
     - Optimization results (2D parameter sweep, 3D surface plot)
     - Trade history table with sort/filter
     - The "Report" tab — what metrics it shows and in what order
     - How it marks trade entries/exits on the price chart
     - INSIGHT QUESTION: MT5 is the most widely used retail platform.
       What do experienced MT5 users wish the Strategy Tester showed
       that it doesn't? What are the known blind spots?

  b) TradingView
     - Strategy Tester panel (equity curve, drawdown, buy & sell markers)
     - Performance Summary tab (net profit, max drawdown, Sharpe, etc.)
     - List of Trades tab (sortable, filterable)
     - How it overlays strategy signals on price charts
     - INSIGHT QUESTION: TradingView has the best chart UX in retail.
       What visualization patterns do they use that make complex data
       feel simple? What can we steal?

  c) cTrader
     - Backtesting results layout
     - Trade statistics panel
     - Long vs. short performance separation
     - INSIGHT QUESTION: cTrader is known for cleaner UX than MT5.
       Where specifically is it better at surfacing insight?

  d) NinjaTrader
     - Strategy Analyzer results
     - Walk-forward optimization display — this is critical, most platforms
       don't have walk-forward visualization at all
     - Monte Carlo simulation views
     - INSIGHT QUESTION: NinjaTrader has the most mature walk-forward
       tooling in retail. How do they present window-by-window results?
       What makes pass/fail obvious?

  e) QuantConnect (LEAN)
     - Browser-based backtest results dashboard
     - Equity curve + benchmark overlay
     - Rolling statistics panels
     - Alpha/beta decomposition views
     - INSIGHT QUESTION: QuantConnect targets quant developers, not
       retail point-and-click traders. What do they show that the
       retail platforms skip? What's the "quant-grade" difference?

  f) Backtrader / Zipline / vectorbt (Python ecosystem)
     - Default plotting output
     - vectorbt's interactive portfolio analytics
     - Tear sheets (pyfolio/quantstats) — what metrics, what layout
     - INSIGHT QUESTION: The Python quant community has strong opinions
       about what matters. What do tear sheets prioritize that platform
       dashboards ignore?

  g) Institutional / hedge fund tools (if accessible)
     - Bloomberg PORT analytics
     - Kdb+/q dashboards used in systematic trading
     - Risk management dashboards (VaR visualization, stress test results)
     - INSIGHT QUESTION: What do institutional desks see that retail
       traders never get? Even if we can't replicate the data, can we
       replicate the LAYOUT and PRESENTATION patterns?

For EACH platform, capture:
  - Screenshot of the primary results screen (first thing you see after backtest)
  - What information is above the fold vs. requires scrolling/clicking
  - How they visualize drawdown (separate chart? overlay? color-coded?)
  - How they handle session/time-of-day analysis (if at all)
  - Walk-forward / out-of-sample visualization (if supported)
  - What insight is EASY to get vs. what requires manual work
  - What's missing that would help the operator make better decisions


2. ANALYTICAL VISUALIZATION PATTERNS — Beyond Line Charts
----------------------------------------------------------
Most backtest dashboards show an equity curve and a table. That's necessary
but not sufficient. Research these specific visualization types and find
the best implementations:

  a) Equity curve overlays
     - Gross vs. net equity (shows cost drag)
     - In-sample vs. out-of-sample shading (shows temporal boundaries)
     - Benchmark overlay (buy-and-hold, risk-free rate)
     - Multiple strategy versions on one chart (comparison)
     - Best examples from any platform or library

  b) Drawdown visualization
     - Underwater plots (time spent below peak)
     - Drawdown duration histograms
     - Recovery analysis (how long to recover from each drawdown)
     - Drawdown vs. trade count scatter (was the drawdown 1 bad trade
       or a slow bleed over 20 trades?)
     - Best examples from any platform or library

  c) Trade distribution analysis
     - P&L histogram with session coloring
     - Time-of-day heatmap (hour vs. day-of-week, colored by P&L)
     - Hold duration distribution
     - MAE/MFE scatter (Maximum Adverse/Favorable Excursion — shows
       how far trades go against you before recovering, critical for
       stop-loss calibration)
     - Best examples from any platform or library

  d) Walk-forward visualization
     - Window-by-window equity curves (small multiples)
     - Pass/fail heatmap across windows
     - Parameter stability across windows (do optimal params drift?)
     - Degradation curves (does performance decay in later windows?)
     - Best examples — NinjaTrader, QuantConnect, academic papers

  e) Regime and session analysis
     - Session P&L breakdown (stacked bars or faceted equity curves)
     - Volatility regime overlay on equity curve
     - Calendar heatmap (day-level P&L, like GitHub contribution graph)
     - Correlation with market conditions (VIX, ATR, spread widening)

  f) Risk visualization
     - Rolling Sharpe ratio (shows strategy stability over time)
     - Rolling win rate
     - Confidence bands on equity curve (Monte Carlo or bootstrap)
     - Risk-of-ruin visualization
     - Tail risk: P&L distribution with fat tail highlighting

  g) Comparison views
     - Strategy leaderboard with sparklines
     - Side-by-side equity curves with synchronized time axes
     - Radar/spider charts for multi-metric comparison
     - Parameter sensitivity heatmaps (which params matter most?)

For each visualization type:
  - Find the best 2-3 real-world implementations (screenshot + source)
  - Note which library/tool was used to create it
  - Assess: is this genuinely useful or just visual noise?
  - How hard is it to build in a browser?


3. CHARTING LIBRARIES — Technical Evaluation
---------------------------------------------
Evaluate these libraries for rendering financial analytics in a browser.
We need libraries that handle both time-series AND statistical charts.

  a) Lightweight-charts (TradingView open-source)
     - Purpose-built for financial time-series
     - Custom overlay support (trade markers, split boundaries)
     - Limitation: no bar charts, heatmaps, scatter plots
     - Can it handle 400K points with smooth interaction?

  b) Apache ECharts
     - Financial chart support (candlestick, line, area)
     - Large dataset mode (progressive rendering, dataZoom)
     - Heatmap and 3D surface support (parameter sweeps)
     - One library for everything? Or jack of all trades?

  c) Plotly.js
     - Financial chart types + statistical charts
     - WebGL renderer for large datasets
     - Subplots and faceting for comparison views
     - Bundle size and loading overhead

  d) D3.js
     - Maximum flexibility, highest development cost
     - Observable Plot as a simpler D3 wrapper
     - techan.js, d3fc for financial charts
     - Only worth it if no other library can do what we need

  e) Highcharts Stock
     - Navigator, range selector, comparison tools
     - Licensing: free for personal use?

  f) uPlot
     - Extreme rendering performance
     - Minimal API — how much custom work needed?
     - Can it do annotations, overlays, markers?

  g) AG Grid (for trade log tables)
     - Virtual scrolling for sortable/filterable trade tables
     - Free vs. enterprise features

  h) visx (Airbnb's React + D3)
     - React-native D3 components
     - Good for custom statistical visualizations?

For each library, evaluate:
  - Rendering performance with 300K+ time-series points
  - Financial chart primitives (candlestick, OHLC, equity line)
  - Annotation/marker support (trade entry/exit, regime boundaries)
  - Heatmap and statistical chart capability
  - React/TypeScript integration quality
  - Bundle size impact on 3-second load target
  - Maintenance status (last release, community health)
  - Can it render the visualizations from Research Area 2?


4. DASHBOARD FRAMEWORKS — Build vs. Adopt
-------------------------------------------
Should we build a custom React SPA or adopt a dashboard framework?

  a) Grafana
     - Used in quant ops for monitoring
     - Plugin ecosystem for custom panels
     - Can it query REST APIs and render custom financial charts?
     - Limitation: how flexible is layout for analytical dashboards?

  b) Streamlit
     - Rapid prototyping, Python-first
     - Layout flexibility for complex multi-chart views?
     - Can it match a custom React app for polish?

  c) Panel (HoloViz)
     - Python-native, Bokeh-based
     - Financial chart support via hvPlot
     - Deployment as standalone app

  d) Dash (Plotly)
     - Mature Python dashboard framework
     - Callback model vs. React component model
     - How do serious quant teams use Dash?

  e) Custom React SPA
     - Full control over UX and insight layout
     - Higher development cost
     - Cherry-pick best charting library per view type

  f) Evidence (evidence.dev)
     - SQL-first analytics framework
     - Markdown-driven dashboards with embedded charts
     - Could work well for batch analysis results

For each, assess:
  - Can it deliver the insight visualizations from Research Area 2?
  - How painful is customization when the defaults don't fit?
  - Is it suited to a single developer maintaining it long-term?
  - Does it handle our data scale (300K+ points) without choking?

WHAT THIS RESEARCH IS NOT
==========================
- Not a tutorial on how to use any library
- Not a prototype build
- Not about backend architecture (already decided: Python FastAPI + REST)
- Not about real-time streaming or live trading monitors
- Not about the data pipeline or how backtests run internally

Focus on: what should the operator SEE to make better decisions, and what's
the best tool to render it.


CONTEXT: WHY THIS MATTERS
==========================
This is a personal trading pipeline, not a SaaS product. One operator, making
real money decisions. The dashboard is the final gate before committing capital.

The operator's current mental model comes from MT5's Strategy Tester. The
dashboard must be AT LEAST as informative as MT5's results tab, but go further:

  - MT5 doesn't show walk-forward results visually
  - MT5 doesn't break down performance by forex session
  - MT5 doesn't overlay cost impact on equity curves
  - MT5 doesn't show regime-dependent performance
  - MT5 doesn't compare strategy versions side by side

These gaps are where we need to be better. The research should find who DOES
do these things well — whether that's institutional tools, Python tear sheets,
or innovative open-source projects — and document exactly how they do it.

The goal is a dashboard where you can look at a strategy for 60 seconds and
know whether it's worth your money.
