# Algorithm Comparison

**CMA-ES (with margin)**: Covariance Matrix Adaptation (CMA-ES) is inherently population-based and easily generates λ candidates per generation, making it well-suited to batch evaluation. The “CMA-ES with Margin” variant (CMAwM) adds a lower bound on discrete variables’ mutation probability, preventing premature convergence on mixed spaces【16†L107-L112】. Benchmarks show that CMAwM *outperforms standard CMA-ES* on mixed-integer tasks【17†L49-L52】. In practice, CMA-ES scales to dozens of dimensions, though it typically requires on the order of hundreds of generations (e.g. budget ≳1000×dim) to converge【59†L205-L210】【53†L63-L66】. CMA-ES does not natively support hierarchical conditionals – all parameters are treated continuously – so conditional variables must be encoded (e.g. using integer encodings or specialized mutation as above). The algorithm is known to be robust (it can be modified to handle noise【56†L1283-L1289】) and maintains diversity via its covariance matrix. In summary, CMA-ES/CMAwM can batch-propose λ candidates (λ tunable by user), but thrives with large budgets and may require careful encoding of discrete/conditional parameters.

**Differential Evolution (DE)**: DE is also population-based and naturally parallel. Variants like “Two-Point DE” generate a full population of trial vectors each generation, making them batch-native. Literature suggests populations on the order of 3–5× the dimensionality often work well【48†L1-L4】 (for 15–30 dims, a few hundred is reasonable). DE handles continuous and integer parameters directly; categorical parameters can be handled by random sampling or encoding. For example, the Optuna DE sampler treats numerics with DE mutations and handles categorical variables by random sampling【34†L38-L43】. No conditional logic is built in – inactive parameters must be manually ignored. DE tends to maintain population diversity well through its mutation/crossover operators, and with enough population size it avoids premature collapse. It can also tolerate noise moderately (the ensemble of candidates averages out some variability), though it has no explicit noise-awareness. Overall, DE (especially a variant like Two-Point DE) is highly parallel-friendly (“excellent … including very high num_workers”【59†L197-L201】) and naturally scales to high dimensions, making it a strong global search method for our use case.

**Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE)**: TPE (e.g. Hyperopt) is a Bayesian method designed for hierarchical search spaces. It *natively supports* conditionals by defining a tree-structured search space【28†L255-L264】. However, TPE is inherently sequential: it proposes one trial at a time and updates its models after each evaluation. Native parallel proposals are not supported out-of-the-box (there is no built-in batch TPE); attempts to parallelize require heuristics or modified versions. Recent issues note that “no existing method” provides truly parallel TPE in Optuna【15†L243-L251】. In a batch setting, one could ask TPE for multiple suggestions in a row (e.g. several `ask()` calls before any `tell()`), but its internal model would not incorporate any of them until told, reducing efficiency. Also, TPE (like other Bayesian optimizers) is known to struggle with **noisy objectives**, since it assumes deterministic evaluations. In short, TPE handles mixed/categorical/conditional spaces gracefully by design【28†L255-L264】, but it is not batch-native (parallel proposals will degrade model updates) and is less robust to high noise than population-based methods.

**Hybrid Approaches**: Combining methods (e.g. DE for exploration followed by CMA-ES for fine-tuning) is conceptually attractive but complex. There is limited literature specifically on hybrid DE + CMA for HPO. One could, for instance, run DE for a number of generations to broadly explore, then initialize CMA-ES around the best found points. Such multi-phase strategies can help balance exploration and exploitation, but they require careful design and are not natively supported in standard libraries. In practice, a fallback strategy is to switch algorithm if one stalls.  

**Batch-Native Suitability Summary**:
- *CMA-ES/CMAwM*: Batch-native (population λ per generation). Quality is maintained in batch mode since it is inherently generation-based.
- *DE*: Fully batch (generate entire population). Strong parallel scaling.
- *TPE*: Not batch-native; parallel suggestions must be “dummy” (model only updates sequentially)【15†L243-L251】.
- *Hybrid*: Depends on orchestration; no standard batch implementation exists.

# Mixed Parameter Types

Our space has continuous, integer, categorical, and hierarchical (conditional) parameters:

- **Continuous/Integer**: CMA-ES and DE handle these directly. CMAwM can treat integers as discrete by adding margin support【16†L107-L112】. DE applies mutation to floats and can round to integers or use special distributions (e.g. geometric steps【56†L1265-L1273】). Both require careful parameter scaling (e.g. mapping integers to ranges with steps). 
- **Categorical**: Neither CMA-ES nor DE natively support categorical values. Common workarounds include one-hot encoding or mapping categories to integer indices, but this breaks the algorithms’ assumptions (e.g. Euclidean distance). For example, the Optuna DE sampler solves this by *randomly sampling* categorical values independently, rather than using DE operators【34†L38-L43】, effectively decoupling categoricals from evolution. TPE/Hyperopt, by contrast, *natively* handles categorical and hierarchical parameters (since the search space can encode conditionals)【28†L255-L264】.
- **Conditional (Hierarchical)**: TPE is explicitly designed for tree-structured search spaces【28†L255-L264】, so it *innately* supports conditionals (inactive branches are simply not considered until parent values permit them). Evolution strategies do not: a conditional parameter is still present in the vector (though its effect should be masked based on its parent variable). One could encode by, e.g., fixing default values or using surrogate splits, but that requires manual handling.

**Encoding Trade-offs**: Converting integers/booleans to continuous (with rounding) or one-hot for categoricals can allow use of CMA/DE but at the cost of search efficiency. Rounding can distort the search landscape and small changes in real values may map to the same integer. One-hot expands dimensionality and can confuse algorithms (e.g. small Hamming-distance moves). In contrast, TPE’s native handling avoids these issues but sacrifices batch parallelism.

In practice, given our heavy parallelism requirement, CMAwM plus smart encodings (or using a hybrid like DE for numeric parts plus random for categoricals) might be necessary. For example, one strategy is to let DE evolve continuous/integer parts while randomly assigning categoricals each generation (as Optuna’s DE sampler does【34†L38-L43】). 

# Ask/Tell Interface and Libraries

We require an *ask-and-tell* interface for batch optimization. Several Python libraries support this:

- **Nevergrad** (Facebook Research): Actively maintained, it supports an ask/tell paradigm for many algorithms. Its `ask()` can be called repeatedly to fill a worker pool (batch mode), then `tell()` inputs scores. The docs confirm batch mode (e.g. `batch_mode=True` asks for `num_workers` points in one go) and recommend population algorithms like TwoPointsDE for high parallelism【59†L197-L201】. Nevergrad’s `CMA` and `TBPSA` are available, but note: TBPSA is a variant aimed at noisy problems【59†L208-L211】. Nevergrad also has mechanisms to “inoculate” known points (via `suggest` and `spawn_child`) for warm-starting. It is relatively mature, with good docs (see its “How to perform optimization” guide【12†L125-L133】). 
- **cycma (PyCMA)**: A pure-Python CMA-ES implementation (CyberAgent’s library). It exposes ask/tell (`CMA.ask()`/`CMA.tell()`) and is stable and well-documented (referencing Hansen’s CMA-ES). It only covers CMA-ES (no DE or TPE). It would need manual handling of failed evaluations (see below) and warm-start (via initial mean, step-size).
- **Optuna**: Primarily trial-based, but since v2.6 it offers an Ask-and-Tell interface. It supports TPE and random samplers, but its parallelism is limited. It also provides a DE sampler via OptunaHub (as we saw), which integrates DE+random sampling【34†L38-L43】. Warm-starting is limited (you can inject fixed trials).
- **SMAC3**: A Bayesian optimization suite with an ask/tell API【39†L0-L5】. It supports parallel evaluation via asynchronous updates, but it’s centered on decision-tree-based BO, not easily extended to CMA/DE. It can handle failures (failed configs) by marking runs unsuccessful. SMAC is actively maintained for hyperparameter tuning, but using DE/CMA inside it would require custom wrappers.
- **PyMOO**: A multi-objective toolkit that also supports single-objective search with DE, CMA-ES, etc. It primarily uses a `solve()` interface, but it can be coaxed into ask/tell by using its lower-level classes. It is actively maintained. It does not natively treat mixed types or conditionals.
- **Other**: Trieste (GP-Bayes) has ask/tell but is GP-based, not suitable for our mixed/high-d noise. HyperOpt’s TPE is more pipeline-based (Trials objects) than ask/tell.

Weighing maturity and ease: **Nevergrad** stands out because it has CMA and DE implemented with ask/tell, supports high parallelism, and offers flexibility. Optuna is popular and has TPE/DE (with ask/tell in recent versions), but mainly sequential (except DE which is hybrid). PyCMA is lightweight for CMA-ES specifically. SMAC3 is robust for BO but less flexible on algorithm choice.

**Failed Evaluations**: If a Rust evaluation crashes, we should handle it without derailing the optimizer. Some strategies: assign a high loss (penalty) so the optimizer avoids that region, or simply skip the candidate. Nevergrad expects a `tell()` for each `ask()`. There is no built-in “skip”; one must either tell a worst-case value or catch exceptions and continue (possibly with fewer `tell()` calls, which may desynchronize budgeting). SMAC counts failures internally (marking configurations as crashed). In practice, we’ll likely wrap each evaluation in try/catch and on failure call `tell(candidate, +∞)` or a large positive score, which effectively removes that candidate.

**Warm-Starting & Checkpointing**: True warm-start (feeding past evaluated points) is rare. Nevergrad allows manually telling points via `suggest/spawn_child`, but otherwise it starts fresh. PyCMA can set initial mean/covariance based on prior runs. Optuna can resume from previous study state (using a DB). For checkpointing, one should serialize the optimizer state: most Python implementations (nevergrad, PyCMA, SMAC) allow pickling or saving internal state. Regularly saving the optimizer object (or study in Optuna) lets us resume after a crash. If not possible, we can treat earlier evaluations as an initial known sample set (though that’s only feasible in “suggest” or by pre-populating models).

# Population Sizing and Budget

Given ~750 evals/sec and wanting to propose ~2048 at once, we can afford large populations. Some heuristics:
- **Population Size**: In DE, a rule of thumb is 5–10× the dimensionality. For 30 dims, that’s ~150–300 individuals. With 2048 available workers, one could even use populations of 500–1000 to fully utilize resources. CMA-ES’s default λ is usually small (∼4+⌊3 log n⌋), but it can be increased. Nevergrad’s DE sampler auto-adjusts population by dimensionality【34†L45-L48】. Starting with a larger population can boost exploration, then consider shrinking it in later phases (or using restarts with larger λ, a known strategy in CMA-ES literature【51†L7-L14】).
- **Exploration vs. Exploitation**: Early on, a large population or high F (DE’s mutation factor) encourages exploration. Later, one can reduce diversity (e.g. by decreasing DE’s F or CMA-ES’s step-size) for exploitation. Given a fixed budget (10K–100K evaluations), one could allocate, say, the first 50–70% of evals for broad search (large pop, high mutation) and the remainder for fine-tuning around the best candidates found.
- **Restarts**: Especially for CMA-ES, restarts with increasing population (IPOP/BOBYQA strategies) are common to escape local optima. With our high-throughput, it may be better to run fewer restarts (each expensive) or just one robust run. However, a few restarts (e.g. 2–3) with randomized initial conditions could improve chance of finding global optima without too much overhead.
- **Adaptive Population**: DE has been studied with adaptive population sizing【48†L1-L4】. One could start with a medium population (e.g. 500) and increase if no improvement is seen, or use nevergrad’s “auto” option as in their DE sampler【34†L45-L48】. For CMA-ES, there are adaptive strategies (e.g. population size adaptation) but these are not standard in basic libraries.

Balancing population and generations: With 10k evals and pop=500, we get 20 generations; if 100k evals, 200 generations. We want enough generations for convergence, but also enough individuals to cover space. A compromise might be pop≈200–300 with 30 dims (giving 50–100 gens at 15k–30k evals). But since we have ~2048 workers, we could oversubscribe: generate multiple generations of 2048 and update as completions come (asynchronous modes), or issue multiple batches sequentially. 

# Convergence Detection and Early Stopping

We need heuristics to stop or restart when improvement stalls. Common signals:
- **Objective Plateau**: If the best score hasn’t improved significantly over many generations (relative to noise level), the run may have converged. For CMA-ES, one can monitor its internal σ (step-size); if σ becomes very small or if the covariance matrix collapses, it signals convergence. Many CMA implementations terminate when σ drops below a threshold.
- **Stagnating Diversity**: If the population variance in parameter space falls below a small epsilon (all candidates become nearly identical), the algorithm is stuck in a basin. One can measure e.g. average pairwise distance among individuals and stop if below a threshold.
- **Budget Exhaustion**: We stop when eval budget is used.
- **Noise-Induced Stagnation**: With our noisy CV objective, small random fluctuations can look like improvements. We should consider smoothing or requiring improvements beyond the expected noise level (e.g. requiring a candidate to beat the current best by more than λ×std_dev of CV).
- **Triggering Restarts**: If stagnation is detected *early* (before budget end) or if diversity collapses too soon, one could restart the optimizer (fresh population) or switch algorithm. For example, if CMA-ES σ → 0 before a satisfactory score, restart with larger initial σ or different mean. Another signal: if the high end of the candidate distribution (rather than the best) isn’t improving, it may be worth a reset.

In practice, we might set a criterion like “no improvement in best CV score after X generations” or “population radius below ε for Y generations” to declare convergence. At that point, we either stop or reinitialize with a new random seed. This prevents wasting budget on negligible gains.

# Benchmarks and Evidence

Literature and benchmarks suggest that **CMA-ES/CMAwM** is highly competitive for high-dimensional, expensive problems. For example, an HPO study found that CMA-ES outperformed many methods when budgets were large【53†L63-L66】. Specifically, “the CMA-ES has shown the best performance among more than 100 optimization methods for various BBO problems with moderate to large evaluation budgets (>100× number of variables)”【53†L63-L66】. Conversely, in low-budget regimes BO methods may excel【53†L68-L72】. For our 10K–100K evals (≈300× dimensions at the low end), CMA-ES is in a favorable regime. DE is known to scale to high dimensions and can outperform CMA-ES on separable problems【57†L1-L4】, and it is often used in practice for robust global search. TPE/Bayesian methods can struggle with noise; in fact, evolutionary strategies can be modified for noise robustness【56†L1283-L1289】, giving them an edge when CV variance is significant.

Specific trading or finance benchmarks are scarce, but evolutionary algorithms (ES/DE) have been used in algorithmic trading parameter tuning. The highly-parallel throughput favors these population methods. Experience in industry (e.g. large firms using Nevergrad or proprietary ES implementations) aligns with choosing such optimizers for many-core HPO.

# Recommendation

**Primary Algorithm**: We recommend **CMA-ES with Margin (CMAwM)** as the primary optimizer. Rationale: it is population-based (fits batch ask/tell), excels with large evaluation budgets (as in our setting)【53†L63-L66】, and the margin variant handles mixed discrete/continuous variables【16†L107-L112】. Its covariance matrix update maintains diversity and can adapt to the search landscape. For noisy objectives, CMA-ES’s inherent strategy is robust (it can be made noise-aware【56†L1283-L1289】) and it will average over population. 

**Fallback/Hybrid Strategy**: A strong fallback is **Differential Evolution (DE)**. DE naturally parallelizes and maintains diversity; it can run with hundreds of parallel candidates. For a hybrid approach, one could run DE initially (for broad exploration of the 30-D space) and then switch to CMA-ES focusing on promising regions. If CMA-ES stalls (e.g. population diversity collapses), a restart with new random seed or DE run could escape. Also, Nevergrad’s TwoPointsDE is explicitly recommended for high-parallel scenarios【59†L197-L201】.

**Python Library**: **Nevergrad** is the recommended framework. It implements CMA-ES (and CMAwM variants via injection of discrete support) and several DE variants (including TwoPointsDE, TBPSA) with an ask/tell interface. Its documentation explicitly highlights TwoPointsDE for high `num_workers` and indicates CMA for large-budget scenarios【59†L197-L202】. It has built-in support for handling some conditionals (via instrumentation), checkpointing via pickling, and continuing after interruptions. For CMA-ES specifically, the `cycma` (PyCMA) package or `pylibcma` can be used if finer control is needed. Optuna with its DE sampler【34†L38-L43】 is an alternative, but it is less flexible for pure batch execution and mixed encoding.

**Configuration Guidelines**: Use a *large population* relative to dimensionality (e.g. 10×–20× dims, or up to hundreds). For example, with 30 dims, start with pop ~300–500. Given 750 eval/s, this allows many generations quickly. If using CMA-ES, set λ accordingly (or use an “auto” scheme). Use a **budget of at least 10K** evals (preferably more) to allow convergence (the literature suggests budgets ≳100× dims for good results【53†L63-L66】; our budgets exceed that). Initially emphasize exploration: high mutation (DE’s F~0.8, cross-rate ~0.7 by default, CMA’s step-size large), then gradually tighten. Maintain population diversity by not letting step-size or mutation factor collapse too early. Consider an *adaptive population*: e.g. start with pop=500 and if no progress after N generations, increase pop or restart. Experiment with strategy parameters in pre-testing (CMA-ES’s μ/λ, or DE’s F/CR) to see what gives stable improvements. 

**Convergence Criteria**: Monitor the population spread and best-score improvement. Stop or restart if, say, the best CV score doesn’t improve by more than a threshold for X generations, or if the standard deviation of candidate scores falls below a noise floor. Use the lambda*std component of the objective to gauge real improvement. Also watch CMA-ES’s σ: if it shrinks extremely, trigger a restart. Given the noise, require improvements beyond expected random fluctuations (e.g. improvements >5% of current score) before declaring genuine progress.

**Known Limitations**: CMA-ES/CMAwM still struggles with very high-dimensional discrete spaces and many conditionals – performance can degrade if too many inactive dimensions. If the CV-noise is extremely high, even CMA-ES can waste budget refining stochastic fluctuations; in that case, TBPSA or other noise-aware variants (e.g. using larger population or averaging evaluations) might help. TPE/Bayesian methods were found inferior in high-dim, batch-noisy settings【53†L68-L72】, so they are not primary here. Finally, all these methods find local optima; they may converge to a suboptimal basin, so multiple restarts or hybridization is advised if results plateau. 

**Citations:** We base these conclusions on optimization literature. CMA-ES is documented to excel in large-budget HPO【53†L63-L66】, and CMAwM effectively handles mixed discrete variables【16†L107-L112】【17†L49-L52】. Two-point DE’s suitability for high-parallel settings is noted by Nevergrad【59†L197-L201】. TPE’s design for conditional spaces is highlighted in the original TPE paper【28†L255-L264】, though it lacks batch proposals【15†L243-L251】. Algorithms like ES are known to be noise-robust and extendable to mixed types【56†L1265-L1273】【56†L1283-L1289】. These inform our recommendation of CMA-ES (with DE backup) and use of libraries like Nevergrad with ask/tell support.
